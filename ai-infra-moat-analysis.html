The Second-Order Compute Trade

Where, inside the AI buildout, a base is still small enough for a 3–10× and the moat is one the giants buy rather than build. A ranked short list, with the kill shot for each.

As of 5 June 2026 Prices: 4 Jun close 5 search angles · 27 names screened Credit data: ICE BofA OAS / FRED

The one-line thesis: the market has already named the optical chokepoint (Lumentum, Coherent) and bid it past the point where the moat can pay you. The un-finished naming is one layer back — in the test-and-timing consumable razors, where the same HBM/accelerator unit-growth leverage exists but the demand is funded by sold-out-into-2027 memory and foundry capex, not by the credit-fragile neocloud tier whose riskiest paper is already quietly cracking.

This document is the whole picture, in order: how the governing worldview held up under evidence → the live credit signal that disciplines the whole screen → the cap-size table → the ranked names with moat / cap-math / kill-shot / catalyst → the famous names that fail → structural gaps → the punchline. 01The worldview, pressure-tested

You asked me to treat your lens as a hypothesis, not gospel. Four planks bent under the evidence — and each one makes the screen stricter, not looser.

What bent REVISE

  • "Hyperscalers are cash-rich, so their demand is safe" — weakening to wrong. BofA puts big-four AI capex at ~94% of operating cash flow after buybacks/dividends; Amazon's 2026 FCF is guided negative, Alphabet's down ~90%, and the group raised $108B of bonds in 2025 (JPMorgan models ~$1.5T of tech debt to come). The "cash-funded" leg is thinner than assumed — so the credit filter must be applied harder, even to names touching a hyperscaler.
  • "Private credit is gating" — confirmed, wrong poster child. It is Blue Owl (OBDC II halted redemptions outright, Feb 2026) and Cliffwater (gated at its 7% cap against 14% requests) — not Blackstone's BCRED, which actually raised its cap and honored 100%.
  • "~80% HBM margins" — exaggerated. Reported figures are 68–75% (SK Hynix 71.8% operating Q1'26; Micron 74.9% gross). Product-level HBM may touch 80%, but blended reality is lower — and Samsung passed HBM4 qual for Nvidia mid-2026, so the compression clock (2H26 → 2027) is real.
  • "No Chinese substitute in power" — half true. Chinese high-power transformer imports went from <1,500 units (2022) to >8,000 (2025). The 5-year-lead-time moat holds only for the largest GSU/HVDC certified units — and where it holds, it's owned by $160–260B large-caps (GE Vernova, Eaton) that fail the cap-math test anyway.

What held, hard CONFIRMED

  • HBM sold out into 2027 — SK Hynix carries a ~3-year forward book; Micron fully sold for 2026.
  • Memory in genuine structural shortage — DRAM contract prices +58–63% QoQ, NAND +70–75% QoQ as wafers cannibalize to HBM (HBM eats ~3× wafer area per bit).
  • HBM4 base die migrates to the foundry — built on TSMC N12 / N3P from 2026, a real value-shift toward the foundry layer.
  • Public-vs-private credit divergence — see the live panel below.

04The ranked short list

Ranked on the product of five things: small-enough base × non-absorbable moat × real revenue now × demand funded by cash/foundry-capex (not neocloud credit) × durability to 2027–2030.

1 FormFactor FORM ~$9.9B highest conviction

The cleanest consumable razor at the smallest viable base.

What they sell
Probe cards — custom MEMS-microspring cards that electrically contact every die on a wafer during test, before packaging (~81% of revenue), plus systems/metrology.
Moat
A true razor-and-blade: each probe card is engineered to one chip design, physically wears out, and is re-bought every product generation. HBM makes it worse — every DRAM die is tested individually and re-validated as a Known-Good-Stack-Die, multiplying touchdowns per stack. HBM/DRAM probe-card revenue grew 4× in FY24. Demand pulls with wafers tested & unique die designs, not tool capex — so it keeps earning through a capex pause.
Absorb test
PASSES Fragmented, application-specific, qualification-locked (~26% share). Broadcom/Nvidia/TSMC have no reason to make probe cards; the memory oligopoly buys them. An ATE giant could acquire, but Technoprobe rivalry caps the prize.
Credit test
PASSES Funded by memory-maker & foundry capex (SK Hynix, Micron, TSMC) — the sold-out-into-2027, cash-rich part of the chain. The cleanest funding profile on the list.
Cap math
$9.9B on ~$840M rev, ~42% GM, fwd P/E ~52. A 3× to ~$30B needs probe-card TAM (~$2.5–3B today) to expand with HBM4 16-Hi/chiplet test intensity while FORM holds share and margins normalize. Tighter than the optical names had — but the base is genuinely small and the leverage is structural.
Kill shot
Technoprobe. It already won ~30% of TSMC's 2nm probe-card qualifications with integrated MEMS-tip production. If it takes leading-edge logic and presses DRAM pricing, FORM's modest ~42% margins compress and the razor becomes a low-margin custom shop. Watch the 2nm logic share split quarterly.
Catalyst
HBM4 ramp (2026) lifts touchdowns/stack; chiplet test intensity compounds through 2027. Tell = quarterly DRAM probe-card revenue prints.

2 SiTime SITM ~$18.7B

A genuine ~90% monopoly the market still files under "components."

What they sell
MEMS silicon timing — oscillators, clocks, resonators that replace legacy quartz. Every AI server, switch, and NIC needs multiple timing devices, and content per box is rising with 800G/1.6T.
Moat
~90% of the MEMS timing market on MEMS-fab process IP quartz incumbents can't match (size/power/vibration/programmability). Datacenter line has posted 7 straight quarters of >100% YoY growth, now >50% of revenue; FY25 +61%. The ~$3.2B Renesas timing acquisition removes the #2 and adds ~$300M revenue at ~70% GM.
Absorb test
PASSES At $18.7B a stretch bolt-on; the MEMS process IP is the hard part. Broadcom/Marvell buy the chips, not the fab know-how.
Credit test
PASSES Content-per-unit on AI servers/switches — funded by the same capex as the accelerators; not a China-commoditized discrete.
Cap math
MEMS is still only ~4–5% of the ~$10B total timing market — long displacement runway. $18.7B → ~$56B (3×) implies ~$3B revenue run-rate at 55%+ GM; rich at ~18× fwd sales but supportable if datacenter keeps compounding and Renesas closes.
Kill shot
Valuation meets a unit-growth stall. ~47× EV/revenue with GAAP losses, and timing is content-per-unit not a wearing consumable — there's no aftermarket to cushion a 2027 server-unit deceleration.
Catalyst
Renesas close (2026) steps revenue + removes a rival; 1.6T switch build-outs raise timing content through 2026–27.

3 MACOM MTSI ~$29.2B

The best mid-cap claim on the InP chokepoint — with a credit-proof base.

What they sell
Compound-semi (indium phosphide) photonics — CW lasers, 200G/lane photodetectors — plus the laser drivers & TIAs in every optical module, on top of a large RF industrial-&-defense business.
Moat
The real optical chokepoint isn't the module (China assembles 7 of top 10) — it's the InP laser chip and analog driver/TIA. MACOM is a credible second InP source behind Lumentum/Coherent, with its own compound-semi fab. Datacenter guided +35–40%.
Absorb test
PARTIAL More supplier than rival to Broadcom/Marvell; an acquisition target, but the InP fab + defense franchise make it an expensive, awkward bite.
Credit test
PASSES — best on list The industrial-&-defense half is government-funded and counter-cyclical: a floor the pure optical names lack if the neocloud tier cracks.
Cap math
$29.2B on ~$1.07B rev, ~56% GM. A clean 3× to ~$90B is a stretch (needs datacenter InP to roughly triple while defense grows slowly). A 2× is the honest base case, 3× the upside if it becomes the qualified second InP source at scale.
Kill shot
It fails to scale InP datacenter fast enough and stays a niche RF/defense name while Lumentum/Coherent keep the laser crown — or industry-wide InP capacity adds (Lumentum +40%/yr) turn today's shortage into a 2027 glut.
Catalyst
Datacenter mix crossing ~30–40% of total through FY26; any formal second-source qual at a hyperscaler optical program.

4 Camtek CAMT ~$8.3B higher risk

Smallest base in advanced-packaging inspection; more multiply room, weaker moat.

What they sell
Inspection/metrology for advanced packaging — post-bond and wafer-level packaging inspection, the yield-control layer for CoWoS / 2.5D / HBM stacking. ~50% AI-related.
Moat
A real niche lead in advanced-packaging inspection riding the CoWoS ramp (TSMC scaling toward ~120–130K wafers/mo by end-2026). But it's equipment, not a consumable — capex-linked, more cyclical — and latest-quarter growth decelerated to ~+2.5%.
Absorb test
RISK KLA is far larger and could press down-market into the niche — the central absorb risk.
Credit test
PASSES Foundry/OSAT capex-funded.
Cap math
At $8.3B it has the most headroom of the metrology trio (vs Onto $13.7B, Nova $16.6B). A 3× to ~$25B is conceivable on a CoWoS / panel-level packaging inspection super-cycle.
Kill shot
KLA enters AP inspection aggressively, or CoWoS overbuilds and 2026 digestion cuts tool orders — equipment names have no consumable floor when the capex pulse pauses. TSMC's panel-level (CoPoS) pilot mid-2026 also re-architects tool requirements.
Catalyst
CoWoS capacity adds through 2026; panel-level packaging tool-requalification cycle 2026–27.

5 Cohu COHU ~$2.6B speculative turnaround

Smallest base / most percentage room — but a turnaround bet, not a quality compounder.

What they sell
Semiconductor test handlers and — the interesting part — device-specific test contactors/sockets booked as recurring revenue (~55% recurring), plus new HBM inspection (Neon) and AI handlers (Eclipse).
Moat
The contactor/spares aftermarket is a real, modest consumable razor tied to final-test volume and parallelism. Smallest base = most percentage room if AI test traction lands.
Absorb test
PARTIAL Fragmented socket market (~10% share, vs Yamaichi/Enplas); a plausible roll-up target, which caps standalone upside.
Credit test
PASSES Test-volume / capex-funded.
Cap math
$2.6B → $8–10B is a clean 3–4× if it returns to profitability through the up-cycle and AI handler/inspection traction offsets legacy auto/industrial drag.
Kill shot
It stays unprofitable through the cycle — AI traction fails to offset legacy weakness, and the multiple has no earnings to stand on.
Catalyst
Return to GAAP profitability + AI data-center handler/inspection design wins over the next 2–4 quarters.

05Quality, but park it — not multiply it

Entegris (ENTG) — ~$21.3B. The highest-quality consumable razor in the whole stack: CMP slurries/pads (pads last 150–400 hours, re-bought continuously), filtration, gases — content-per-wafer rising every node, with process-qualification lock-in a giant can't easily absorb. But growth is only ~+5% because it's tied to all wafer starts, not AI specifically, so the AI leverage is diluted; net debt ~$3.3B. This is where you park capital at quality — it won't 3× on this horizon. Kill shot: slurry margin compression vs Asian chemical majors.

Advantest (Tokyo) — large-cap. The HBM-test near-monopoly (60–70% memory ATE), and the test-time explosion (a Blackwell-class part sits >20 min on a tester vs. seconds for commodity silicon) is the single strongest unit-leveraged, capex-funded demand thesis I found. But it is the giant, it's Tokyo-listed, and the base is too big for a 3–10×. Kill shot: Teradyne's Magnum 7H attacking HBM memory test. The toll, not the multiplier.

07Structural gaps — great razor, no clean pure-play

Some of the best consumable economics in the stack have no investable small-cap. Worth knowing so you don't hunt a ghost:

  • Electronic specialty gases (per-wafer consumed) — locked up by the Linde / Air Liquide / Air Products oligopoly.
  • Photoresist (per-exposure consumed) — a Japanese-major lock (JSR >22%, Tokyo Ohka, Shin-Etsu).
  • SiC/quartz chamber consumables (focus rings replaced every 2–3 weeks) — CoorsTek (private), Hana (Korea), Ferrotec.
  • The InP wafer itself — a ~$200M market of private/foreign players. Play it only via integrated chip names (MACOM = best mid-cap fit).

And the truest "before it's named" plays that sit outside a US screen: Technoprobe (Milan — the one taking FORM's share; a natural long/short pair), Besi & ASMPT (hybrid bonding into HBM4 — note AMAT already owns ~9% of Besi, an absorb-by-stake tell), and Disco (dicing blades = a CoWoS-volume consumable).

08The punchline

Where consensus is most wrong

Consensus has crowded into the optical chokepoint (Lumentum/Coherent) at $73–82B because Nvidia's $4B prepay made it un-missable — and in doing so it has bid the moat past the point where the moat can pay you. The un-finished naming is one layer back, in the test-and-timing consumable razors — probe cards (FormFactor), MEMS timing (SiTime), test contactors (Cohu) — where the same HBM/accelerator unit-growth leverage exists, but the demand is funded by sold-out-into-2027 memory and foundry capex rather than by the credit-fragile neocloud tier whose CCC paper is already quietly widening.

If forced to one name: FormFactor. Smallest viable base, the only true wear-and-replace consumable tied directly to HBM unit and chiplet test intensity, funded by the most durable buyers in the chain. Its kill shot is equally specific and worth monitoring quarterly: Technoprobe's share gains at the leading-edge logic node. Win that, and the 3× is physical.

The timing cluster you can't ignore. Every 3× above assumes the AI-capex cycle doesn't air-pocket before 2027 — and the CCC-spread widening flagged in §02 is the first instrument that would tell you it's starting. The memory-margin compression clock (Samsung HBM4 qual, mid-2026) argues the easy money in this whole complex is a 2026 story, with 2027 the year the scarcity premiums — in HBM, in optics, in test — all face their test simultaneously. Size positions to respect that clustering.

Methodology & how to read the verdicts

Built by decomposing the question into five stack-spanning search angles (interconnect/optical; packaging/test/metrology; power/thermal; credit & HBM economics; materials/EDA/niche), running parallel web-research agents on each, then cross-checking load-bearing claims for convergence across independent agents (the corroboration standard). Market caps and fundamentals were pulled from a live finance tool (prices 4 Jun 2026); credit spreads from ICE BofA OAS series via FRED. Each name was put through two binary filters — (A) can a giant absorb the niche? and (B) is demand credit-funded/fragile or capex/cash-funded? — plus a base-size test for whether a 3–10× is physically possible. A name fails if it flunks either filter or is already too large.
