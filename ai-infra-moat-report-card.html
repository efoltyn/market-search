  • Jun 22–25Memory melt-up, then the scare. Micron blew out (record $41.5B rev, 84.6% GM) and Korea capex datapoints lit the memory-WFE complex — then MU sold off on reports Nvidia may halve HBM per rack on Rubin NVL72 + SK Hynix slowing HBM4. First crack in the HBM-volume story.

  • Jun 26Rotation + mechanics. "HBM/DRAM price hikes squeeze hyperscaler margins" narrative → sell the picks-and-shovels; OpenAI signals IPO delay to 2027 after SpaceX's wild debut; and it was the Russell reconstitution effective date — the 3–7× volume that unwound FORM's index-add spike and every high-beta name at once.

03The big miss, explained: capacity-add pays the toolmaker first

What I got wrong: I tagged Ichor (+32%) and Ultra Clean (+27%) as "picks-and-shovels in name, capex-beta in cash flow — avoid." They were the two best names in the entire universe.

Why it happened: June priced a memory-capacity-add cycle — Micron raising FY26 capex to ~$27B, Samsung pulling forward the P4 fab, SK Hynix adding wafers, SEMI guiding 300mm WFE +18% in 2026. A greenfield memory cycle means new tool and subsystem orders — exactly ICHR/UCTT (gas & fluid delivery to Lam/AMAT), KLIC (HBM bonding), ONTO (HBM metrology), ENTG (materials per wafer-start). Their thin-margin "assembler" character amplifies the move via operating leverage, which is why sell-side targets jumped 30–40% in three weeks.

The lesson: a consumable razor (FORM probe cards, COHU contactors) is a production-volume play — it gets paid when the new fabs run, which lags the capacity-add wave by quarters. My razor thesis wasn't wrong; it was at the wrong phase of the cycle for this particular quarter. Right altitude for 2027–2030, wrong altitude for June 2026.

04Where the world proved me wrong on the worldview

HBM margins: I "corrected" 80% down to 68–75%. That correction was wrong. MY BIGGEST MISS

Micron's fiscal-Q3 (24 Jun) printed 84.6% gross margin and guided Q4 to ~86%, sold out through 2027 and into 2028, with HBM4 booked through 2027. The original worldview's "~80% scarcity premium" was, if anything, low. Memory pricing power is stronger and more durable than I credited — and the margin-compression clock I put at 2H26→2027 has been pushed out. The compression trigger did arrive on schedule (all three vendors qualified on HBM4, confirmed 5 Jun) — but demand absorbed the supply, and Micron is locking in take-or-pay contracts whose floor margins it says sit "beyond past-cycle peaks." Delayed, not invalidated — but I was on the wrong side of it.

Credit audit layer: tracking, and quietly accelerating. BEST CALL

The exact bifurcation I flagged widened: CCC OAS 9.41% → 9.68% (fresh high) while IG (0.76%) and HY (2.78%) stayed pinned at tights. The driver was idiosyncratic CCC stress — the First Brands "Ponzi" bankruptcy, Fed-on-hold-into-2027, rising healthcare/retail distress — not yet a neocloud event. But the neoclouds sold off anyway (CoreWeave −18% in eight sessions) on depreciation/concentration/refi fears, even as S&P revised CoreWeave's credit outlook to positive. That equity-vs-credit divergence is the new thing to watch. Private-credit gating keeps broadening (BDC redemptions +217% q/q). Every fragility flag intact; none broken.

06What I'd change

The verdict, unhedged

  • The avoid screen earns a B+. The book I told you to avoid fell −6.4% vs −1.1% for the picks, and the four worst names in the universe were all flagged avoids. As a "don't own these into a de-rating" call, it worked — even though the moats didn't crack; valuations did.
  • The pick book earns a C. One real win (COHU, on the exact catalyst I named), one wild-but-flat index-mechanics name (FORM, thesis intact), two beta losses with intact theses (SITM, MTSI), and one genuinely bad pick (CAMT — too high-beta and too richly valued to own through a rout, even with no bad news).
  • The thesis earns an Incomplete — correctly. Three weeks can't judge a 2027–2030 moat call. But it already taught two things: (1) I under-weighted where in the capex cycle we are — capacity-add rewarded the toolmakers I dismissed before the consumable razors I favored; (2) I over-corrected the HBM-margin call and should have trusted the original scarcity-premium read.

If I rewrote it today: keep the avoid discipline; add ICHR/UCTT-style memory-WFE leverage as the early-cycle complement to the consumable razors (own the toolmaker into the capacity build, rotate to the razor as fabs ramp); raise the memory-pricing-power conviction; and keep the credit-audit lens exactly as is — it's the one part that's calling the tape in real time. The single chart that matters next: CCC OAS vs the first genuine neocloud refi headline. That's where the thesis lives or dies.
